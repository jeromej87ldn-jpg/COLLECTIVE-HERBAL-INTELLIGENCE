Ashwagandha
Withania somnifera
Traditional names: Ashwagandha (Sanskrit, “smell of the horse”) · Indian Winter Cherry · Indian Ginseng
✓ Generally safe Ayurveda Root used Origin: India
🌿 Common name
Ashwagandha — also Indian Winter Cherry, Indian Ginseng
📜 Traditional name
Ashwagandha (Sanskrit) · Asgandh (Unani)
🔬 Botanical name
Withania somnifera · family Solanaceae
🗺 Location
Native to India, Nepal, and parts of North Africa and the Middle East; now cultivated widely in dry regions.
🌱 Part(s) used
Root (primary)Leaf (topical/traditional)
🏷 Type
AdaptogenNervineRasayana (rejuvenative)
🕰 History
One of the most revered herbs in Ayurveda, used for over 3,000 years as a rasayana — a rejuvenating tonic believed to restore vitality and longevity. Its Sanskrit name, “smell of the horse,” points both to the root’s scent and to the traditional idea that it imparts the strength and stamina of a stallion. It travelled through Unani medicine and folk traditions across the Middle East and Africa before reaching modern Western herbalism.
💚 Benefits to the body
Traditionally used to support the body’s resilience to stress and to promote calm, steady energy. It is also traditionally used to support restful sleep, healthy stamina, and a settled mind. Research suggests it may support a balanced stress response. (Language kept MHRA-compliant — “traditionally used / may support / research suggests”.)
⚖️ Dosages
Root powder: 3–6 g daily.
Standardised extract: 300–600 mg daily.
Tincture: 2–4 ml, up to 3× daily.
🫖 Preparation
Powder stirred into warm milk with a little honey (the classic Ayurvedic moon milk), capsules, or tincture. Gentle simmering as a decoction suits the root.
⏰ Timings
Often taken in the evening for its calming, sleep-supportive use; a morning dose suits those using it for daytime steadiness. Commonly cycled (e.g. 6 weeks on, 1–2 off).
⚠️ Cautions
Best avoided in pregnancy. Use with care alongside sedatives, thyroid medication, or immunosuppressants, and with autoimmune or thyroid conditions. Part of the nightshade family — relevant for sensitive individuals. Seek professional guidance if unsure.
